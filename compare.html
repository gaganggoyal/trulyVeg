The Swap Guide

Why this, not that — in detail

Fourteen everyday products where “looks veg” and “is veg” part ways. Each comparison covers what it’s made from, how it’s made, health notes, taste, cost — and exactly what to buy instead, in plain generic terms so you can find it under any brand. No hand-waving; read the evidence and decide.

Swap 01

Synthetic Vinegar vs Naturally Fermented Vinegar

The bottle says “vinegar”. But one is food that fermented; the other is an industrial chemical that got diluted. Both are technically vegetarian — only one was ever food.

❌ Synthetic “White Vinegar”✅ Naturally Fermented Vinegar
What it isAcetic acid (often from petrochemical feedstock or wood pulp processing) diluted to ~4–7% with waterSugarcane juice, apples, coconut sap or jamun fermented to alcohol, then to vinegar by acetobacter
How it’s madeChemical synthesis + dilution; hoursTwo-stage natural fermentation; weeks to months
What’s insideAcetic acid + water. Nothing else — no minerals, no enzymes, no complexityAcetic acid plus malic/citric acids, trace minerals, polyphenols from the source fruit; raw versions carry the live “mother”
TasteFlat, harsh, one-note sournessRounded acidity with the aroma of its source — sugarcane sirka tastes of sugarcane
Traditional useNone — a 20th-century industrial shortcutSirka is centuries old in Indian kitchens; vinegar-making is older than written recipes
Cost₹30–₹50 / 500 ml₹90–₹250 / 500 ml — you use less because it tastes of something
Label tellIngredients: “acetic acid, water”Ingredients: “fermented sugarcane juice” / “apple juice”; words brewed or naturally fermented
Verdict: A pickle preserved in synthetic acid is not the achaar your grandmother made. Pay the extra ₹60 — for pickling, salads and health tonics, use only naturally fermented vinegar. Where to buy →
Swap 02

Gelatin vs Agar-Agar & Pectin

The wobble in jelly, the chew in gummies, the shell of your capsule — very often gelatin. Most people who eat it daily have never been told what it is.

❌ Gelatin✅ Agar-Agar / Pectin
SourceCollagen extracted by boiling animal skin, bones, hides and connective tissue (cattle, pigs, fish)Agar: red seaweed. Pectin: apple pomace and citrus peel
Veg status❌ Never vegetarian✅ Always plant-based
Hides inJelly, marshmallows, gummy bears, gummy vitamins, mousse cakes, some ice creams and yogurts, capsule shells, marshmallow fondantUsed openly: veg jelly crystals, jams, pectin gummies, falooda, panna cotta
PerformanceMelts near body temperature (the “melt-in-mouth” wobble)Agar sets firmer and holds at room temperature — better for Indian summers; pectin gives the classic jam set
Cooking notesBloomed in cold water, dissolved warmAgar must be boiled to activate; 1 tsp agar powder ≈ 8 tsp gelatin in setting power
Label tell“Gelatin, gelatine, E441, hydrolysed collagen”“Agar (E406)”, “pectin (E440)” — both always veg
Verdict: There is no culinary job gelatin does that agar or pectin cannot. Check every gummy, jelly and capsule — and swap without compromise.
Swap 03

Animal-Rennet Cheese vs Vegetarian Cheese & Paneer

Cheese needs an enzyme to set. Where that enzyme comes from decides whether your “vegetarian” pizza actually is.

❌ Animal-Rennet Cheese✅ Vegetarian-Rennet Cheese / Paneer
The enzymeRennet (chymosin) scraped from the fourth stomach of slaughtered unweaned calvesMicrobial rennet (from fungi) or fermentation-produced chymosin (FPC); paneer needs none — just lemon or curd
Common examplesTraditional Parmigiano-Reggiano, Grana Padano, Pecorino, Gorgonzola (animal rennet is required by their PDO recipes), many imported cheddarsNearly all Indian-made cheese, paneer, most branded mozzarella in India, cheeses labelled “vegetarian rennet”
Taste & function—Identical. FPC is the same enzyme, made by fermentation — most industrial cheese worldwide already uses it
Label tell“Rennet”, “animal enzymes”, or just “enzymes” with no source on imported cheese“Microbial rennet”, “non-animal enzymes”, “suitable for vegetarians”, green dot in India
The restaurant trapA “veg” pasta finished with imported parmesan is usually not vegetarian. Ask, or request Indian hard cheese instead.
Verdict: In India you’re mostly safe; with imports, “enzymes” of unnamed origin means assume animal rennet. Paneer remains the world’s most honest cheese.
Swap 04

Fish Oil vs Algal Omega-3

❌ Fish Oil Capsules✅ Algal Oil (Plant DHA/EPA)
SourcePressed from anchovies, sardines, cod liver — plus a gelatin softgel shellMicroalgae (Schizochytrium) grown in tanks — the original producers of DHA/EPA that fish merely accumulate
The nutrientsDHA + EPAThe same DHA + EPA, molecule for molecule
PurityOcean-dependent: requires purification for mercury, PCBs, microplasticsGrown in controlled tanks — no ocean contaminants, no fishy burps
Note on flaxseedFlax, chia and walnuts give ALA, which the body converts to DHA/EPA only partially (~5–10%). Great daily foods — but for therapeutic omega-3 levels, algal oil is the direct veg source.
Verdict: “Omega-3 requires fish” is simply outdated. Go to the algae the fish went to.
Swap 05

Lanolin / Fish-Liver Vitamin D3 vs Lichen D3

❌ Standard D3 Supplements✅ Lichen-Derived D3 (+ Sunlight)
SourceCholecalciferol from lanolin (grease in sheep’s wool) or cod liver oil — rarely declared on the frontCholecalciferol extracted from lichen — chemically identical, certified vegan
Hidden inFortified foods, multivitamins, D3 sachets — often inside a gelatin softgel tooClearly labelled “vegan/plant-based D3”
The free option20–30 minutes of midday sun on bare arms remains the most vegetarian vitamin D there is. Supplements fill the gap, not replace the sun.
Verdict: Same molecule, cleaner source. If the label doesn’t say the D3 source, assume lanolin or fish.
Swap 06

Carmine (E120) vs Plant Colours

❌ Carmine / Cochineal (E120)✅ Beetroot, Annatto & Friends
Source~70,000 cochineal insects crushed per pound of dyeBeetroot (E162), annatto seeds (E160b), curcumin (E100), anthocyanins from grapes and berries (E163)
Hides inPink/red yogurts, strawberry milk, candies, red velvet items, lipsticks, “natural red 4” — “natural colour” does not mean plant colourClearly named plant extracts on honest labels
PerformanceVery stable redBeet reds are heat-shy (add late); anthocyanins shift with pH — bakers manage both easily
Verdict: If a red or pink product doesn’t name its colour source, ask. E120 is one of the most common accidental non-veg ingredients in the world.
Swap 07

Refined White Sugar vs Jaggery & Khandsari

⚠️ Refined White Sugar✅ Jaggery (Gur) / Khandsari
ProcessingHeavily refined; in some countries (parts of the US, notably) filtered through bone char from cattle bones. Indian sugar generally uses sulphitation instead — veg, but chemically bleachedOpen-pan boiled cane juice (gur) or minimally refined crystals (khandsari) — no bone char, no bleaching
What survivesPure sucrose; every mineral strippedIron, magnesium, potassium traces and cane’s own molasses flavour
Ayurvedic viewRefined sugar is considered tamasic — dullingOld gur (purana gur) is the classic sattvic sweetener
Honest noteSugar is sugar metabolically — jaggery is a cleaner, more traceable sweetener, not a health food. Less of either is the real win.
Verdict: For India the issue is processing chemicals and tradition, not bone char — but travellers and NRI readers should know the US bone-char practice. Gur wins on every count that matters.
Swap 08

Gelatin Capsules vs Veg (HPMC) Capsules

❌ Gelatin Capsule✅ HPMC / Pullulan Capsule
Shell materialGelatin — boiled animal skin and bone (see Swap 02)HPMC: plant cellulose. Pullulan: fermented tapioca starch
Where you meet itMost softgels; many hard capsules, including on herbal and “ayurvedic” products — the irony writes itselfAny product printing “veg capsule” or carrying the green dot on the strip
PerformanceStandard dissolutionEquivalent dissolution; better in humid climates (doesn’t soften)
What to doAsk your pharmacist: “Is there a tablet or veg-capsule version?” For most medicines, there is. For supplements, buy only brands that state the shell material.
Verdict: The medicine inside may be veg — the shell often isn’t. One question at the pharmacy fixes it.
Swap 09

Synthetic & Insect Colours vs Natural Plant Colours

Every colour a factory makes, a plant already made first. One route runs through petroleum and crushed insects; the other runs through your spice box. Both can end up the same shade of red — only one was ever food.

❌ Synthetic & Insect Colours✅ Natural Plant & Spice Colours
What it isPetroleum/coal-tar azo dyes (tartrazine, sunset yellow, allura red, ponceau, carmoisine, brilliant blue) and carmine (E120), which is crushed cochineal insectsPigments extracted from food: turmeric, annatto, beetroot, hibiscus, butterfly-pea, spinach, saffron, cocoa
How it’s madeIndustrial synthesis from petroleum by-products; or ~70,000 insects boiled per pound of carminePressed, dried or steeped from the plant — the way a beetroot stains your fingers
Health noteSix azo dyes carry an EU child “activity & attention” warning; several are banned as food colours in the USAWhole-food pigments — many are antioxidants (curcumin, anthocyanins) in their own right
TasteTasteless — pure decorationOften adds gentle flavour and aroma (turmeric, saffron, cocoa)
The colour mapYellow → turmeric, saffron, marigold   Orange → annatto, carrot   Red / pink → beetroot, hibiscus, pomegranate   Blue → butterfly-pea, spirulina   Green → spinach, matcha   Brown → caramelised jaggery, cocoa
Label tellE102, E110, E122, E124, E129, E133, E120, or vague “added colours / natural colour”A named plant: “beetroot extract”, “curcumin (E100)”, “annatto (E160b)”
Verdict: If a colour doesn’t name a plant, treat it as synthetic or insect until proven otherwise. And remember the illegal ones — metanil yellow, lead chromate, Sudan dyes, rhodamine B, malachite green — that appear on loose, unbranded food with no label at all. See the full colour list →
Swap 10

Vanaspati & Refined Oils vs Cold-Pressed Oil & Pure Ghee

The fat you cook in is the fat you become. Here “vegetarian” is not the question — “was this made by pressing a seed, or by a chemical plant?” is.

❌ Vanaspati & Refined/Deodorised Oil✅ Cold-Pressed Oil & Pure Ghee
What it isVanaspati is vegetable oil hardened by hydrogenation; refined oils are solvent-extracted, bleached and steam-deodorisedCold-pressed (kachi ghani / wood-pressed) oil crushed at low heat; ghee is clarified cultured butter
How it’s madeSeeds washed in a hexane-type solvent, then bleached and heated to strip colour and smellSeed or nut crushed in a press; nothing added, nothing extracted with chemicals
Health noteHydrogenation creates trans fats (now legally capped, but old stock and street food remain); deodorising destroys aroma and antioxidantsKeeps the oil’s natural vitamin E, aroma and flavour; ghee is a classic sattvic fat in Ayurveda
Hides inCheap “ghee” & blended ghee, bakery & biscuits, cream fillings, chocolate coatings, fried namkeen, non-dairy whipNamed single-source oils (mustard, groundnut, sesame, coconut) and single-ingredient ghee
Label tell“Hydrogenated / partially hydrogenated vegetable oil”, “vanaspati”, “edible vegetable oil (refined)”“Cold-pressed / kachi ghani / wood-pressed”; ghee ingredients: just “cow milk / butter”
Verdict: Vanaspati wearing the word “ghee” is one of the oldest tricks in the market. Read the ingredients, not the picture on the tin — real ghee lists one thing, and cold-pressed oil names its seed.
Swap 11

Analogue “Paneer” vs Real Milk Paneer

A curious modern fake: a white block that looks like paneer but has barely met a cow. It isn’t “non-veg” — it’s something worse for a pure kitchen, a synthetic imitation of real food.

❌ Analogue / Filler Paneer✅ Real Milk Paneer
What it isStarch, refined or palm oil, milk powder and emulsifiers pressed into a paneer-shaped block — little or no fresh milkFresh milk curdled with lemon, curd or citric acid, then pressed. Two ingredients
Why it existsIt’s far cheaper — which is why it turns up in bulk catering, cheap restaurant gravies and some frozen blocksIt’s honest food — the reason India invented it as its everyday vegetarian protein
How to tellRubbery, over-soft or oddly stretchy; oily aftertaste; doesn’t crumble; stays intact when overcookedCrumbles between fingers, browns when fried, tastes of milk; a drop of iodine turns starchy fakes blue-black
Where it hides“Paneer” dishes priced too low to be real; frozen “paneer” with long ingredient lists; some pizza “cheese”Same-day dairy paneer; or made at home in 20 minutes
Label tellIngredients list starch, “edible vegetable oil”, emulsifiers — or the words “analogue / filler”Ingredients: “milk, acidulant (citric acid/lemon)” and nothing else
Verdict: When paneer is suspiciously cheap, ask. Real paneer cannot cost less than the milk it takes to make it. Where to buy real paneer →
Swap 12

Synthetic Vanilla (Vanillin) vs Natural Vanilla

Most “vanilla” you have tasted never came near the orchid. Synthetic vanillin is vegetarian — but it is a single lab-made molecule standing in for a pod with hundreds.

❌ Vanillin / “Vanilla Flavour”✅ Natural Vanilla Extract & Pods
What it isA single synthesised molecule, historically made from wood-pulp waste (lignin) or petroleum-derived guaiacolCured pods of the vanilla orchid — hundreds of aroma compounds, not one
The “natural flavour” trap“Natural vanilla flavour” is a vague term; in the past, some has been linked to castoreum (a beaver secretion) — the reason unnamed “natural flavours” are never fully resolved“Vanilla extract” or “vanilla bean/pod” names exactly what it is — plant, no ambiguity
Hides inMost ice cream, biscuits, cakes, custard, chocolate and “vanilla” drinks labelled vanilla flavourProducts stating vanilla extract or showing vanilla-bean specks
Label tell“Vanillin”, “vanilla flavour”, “nature-identical flavour”, “added flavour”“Vanilla extract”, “vanilla beans/pods”, “ground vanilla”
Verdict: For a pure kitchen, choose named vanilla extract or a whole pod. If a label just says “flavour”, you are almost certainly tasting a factory, not a flower.
Swap 13

Sugar-Syrup “Honey” vs Raw Honey & Plant Syrups

Two separate questions meet in the honey jar. Is it real, or is it sugar syrup pretending? And — for those who avoid all animal products — is honey even something you want at all? Here are both answers.

❌ Adulterated “Honey”✅ Raw Honey / Plant Syrups
What it isHoney stretched — sometimes mostly replaced — with invert sugar, rice or corn syrup engineered to pass basic testsRaw honey: single-source, unheated, traceable. Or plant syrups: date, jaggery, cane, maple, agave
The veg / vegan lineHoney is accepted by most lacto-vegetarians but excluded by vegans and many Jain readers as a bee product. Plant syrups sidestep the question entirely — a useful swap either way.
How to tell (real honey)Dissolves instantly in water, never crystallises, one-note oversweetness, unusually cheapRaw honey clouds & settles in cold water, may crystallise in winter, carries a floral aroma
Hides inCheap bulk “honey”, honey cough drops, honey-glazed cereals & snacksSingle-origin raw honey; or date/jaggery syrup drizzled where honey would go
Verdict: If you keep honey, buy it raw and single-source. If you avoid it, date and jaggery syrups give the same golden sweetness with none of the doubt.
Swap 14

Refined Free-Flow Salt vs Rock & Sea Salt

A smaller swap, but a true one to the same principle: the less a food is stripped and treated, the closer it stays to what it was.

⚠️ Refined Free-Flow Salt✅ Rock Salt & Sun-Dried Sea Salt
What it isSalt refined to almost pure sodium chloride, then given free-flow and anti-caking agents so it never clumpsRock salt (sendha namak) mined from ancient deposits; sea salt evaporated from seawater by sun and wind
What survivesTrace minerals stripped in refiningNatural trace minerals; rock salt is the traditional choice for vrat/fasting food
Honest noteIodine matters for health, and iodised salt helped end goitre — so this is “minimally processed and natural”, not “never use iodised salt.” Many households simply keep both: iodised for daily cooking, rock/sea salt for its mineral character.
Label tell“Free-flow iodised salt”, anti-caking agent listed“Rock salt / sendha namak”, “sun-dried sea salt”, single ingredient
Verdict: Not a veg-vs-non-veg question — a natural-vs-industrial one. Keep rock or sea salt in the kitchen for the same reason you keep whole spices: it is closer to the source.
