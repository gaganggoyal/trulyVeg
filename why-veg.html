The Foundation

Why vegetarian?
Five thousand years of answers.

Vegetarian living in India is not a diet trend — it is a philosophy with scriptural roots, an Ayurvedic science of food, and now a growing body of modern research behind it. This page walks through all three: what the shastras say, what Ayurveda teaches, and what studies show.

From the Shastras

What the ancient texts teach

The thread running through the Vedas, Upanishads, Smritis and the Gita is ahimsa — the refusal to cause suffering — and the idea that food shapes consciousness. Translations below are widely used renderings; we cite each source so you can study further.

मा हिंस्यात् सर्वभूतानि

“Do not harm any living being.”

Foundational ahimsa maxim, cited across Vedic literature

This four-word instruction is treated by many commentators as the seed of the entire vegetarian tradition — a universal rule, not one limited to ritual contexts.

अघ्न्या — “she who must not be slain”

Across the Rigveda and Yajurveda, the cow is repeatedly addressed as aghnya — the one who must not be killed.

Epithet used throughout Vedic literature (e.g. Rigveda 1.164.40)

Milk, ghee and grain form the Vedic ideal of nourishment that takes no life — the original template of the Indian vegetarian kitchen.

यजुर्वेद १२.३२ — पशून् पाहि

“You must not use your God-given body for killing God’s creatures — neither human, nor animal, nor any living thing.”

Yajurveda 12.32 (widely used rendering)
अहिंसा परमो धर्मस्तथाहिंसा परो दमः । अहिंसा परमं दानम् अहिंसा परमं तपः ॥

“Ahimsa is the highest dharma, the highest self-control, the highest gift, the highest penance.”

Mahabharata, Anushasana Parva 116

The Anushasana Parva contains the Mahabharata’s most extended discussion of food ethics, concluding that abstaining from flesh is the conduct of the wise.

नाकृत्वा प्राणिनां हिंसां मांसमुत्पद्यते क्वचित् । न च प्राणिवधः स्वर्ग्यस्तस्मान्मांसं विवर्जयेत् ॥

“Flesh can never be obtained without injury to living creatures, and injury to living creatures obstructs the path to bliss — therefore let one abstain from flesh.”

Manusmriti 5.48

Manusmriti 5.49 continues: having considered where meat comes from and the bondage of creatures, one should give up all flesh. Verse 5.53 adds that the one who abstains earns the same merit as a hundred ashvamedha rituals.

आयुःसत्त्वबलारोग्यसुखप्रीतिविवर्धनाः । रस्याः स्निग्धाः स्थिरा हृद्या आहाराः सात्त्विकप्रियाः ॥

“Foods that increase life, purity, strength, health, joy and cheerfulness — juicy, wholesome, substantial and agreeable — are dear to the sattvic.”

Bhagavad Gita 17.8

The Gita’s food chapter (17.8–17.10) describes three kinds of food: sattvic (fresh, nourishing — grains, fruits, milk, ghee), rajasic (excessively hot, sour, stimulating) and tamasic (stale, putrid, impure). Vegetarian whole food is the sattvic ideal — and note that the Gita would also count stale, over-processed and synthetic food as tamasic, even if it is technically veg.

தன்னூன் பெருக்கற்குத் தான்பிறிது ஊனுண்பான் எங்ஙனம் ஆளும் அருள்?

“How can one possess kindness, who eats the flesh of another to swell his own flesh?”

Tirukkural 251 — classical Tamil tradition
From Around the World

The same thread runs through every civilisation

Having spent a lifetime walking through the markets, temples and libraries of country after country, I stopped seeing vegetarianism as one culture’s rule. Not every tradition below is fully vegetarian — but in every one of them, from ancient Greece to the Far East to the deserts of the Middle East, the same teaching surfaces: reverence for life, and restraint at the table. Here is the wisdom of the world, in its own voices.

Ἑλλάς — Ancient Greece · Pythagoras & the philosophers

“As long as men massacre animals, they will kill one another. Indeed, one who sows the seed of murder and pain cannot reap joy and love.”

Attributed to Pythagoras (6th c. BCE), preserved in later classical writing

The original “Pythagorean diet” was a meat-free one. Centuries later Plutarch’s essay On the Eating of Flesh and Porphyry’s On Abstinence from Animal Food argued the same case on grounds of health, kinship with animals and clarity of mind — the West’s own ancient vegetarian canon.

जैन आगम — Jain Āgama · Ācārāṅga Sūtra

“All living beings wish to live, not to die; therefore the wise refrain from taking any life. To all things life is dear.”

Ācārāṅga Sūtra 1.4 — earliest Jain scripture

Jainism carries ahimsa to its furthest edge — non-violence to even the smallest creature — and gives the world its clearest argument that a compassionate life begins on the plate.

धम्मपद — Dhammapada · The Buddha’s path

“All tremble at violence; all fear death. Seeing others as like oneself, one should neither kill nor cause to kill.”

Dhammapada 129 — with the First Precept, ahimsa

The first of the Five Precepts is to abstain from taking life. The Mahāyāna Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra goes further, with the Buddha counselling his followers to give up the eating of meat out of compassion for all beings.

בְּרֵאשִׁית — Torah / Hebrew Bible · The first diet

“Behold, I have given you every herb bearing seed… and every tree, in which is the fruit of a tree yielding seed; to you it shall be for food.”

Genesis 1:29

The Book of Daniel (1:12–16) tells of Daniel asking to be fed only “pulse and water” for ten days — and appearing healthier than those who ate the king’s meat: perhaps history’s first recorded dietary trial. Proverbs 12:10 adds, “A righteous man regards the life of his animal.”

道德經 — Tao Te Ching · The three treasures

“I have three treasures which I hold and keep. The first is compassion; the second, frugality; the third, humility.”

Tao Te Ching 67 — attributed to Lao Tzu

From this compassion (cí) grew the East-Asian practice of fàngshēng — releasing captive animals to spare their lives — and centuries of vegetarian cooking in the monastery kitchens of China, Korea and Japan.

القرآن — Qur’an · Communities like your own

“There is not an animal on the earth, nor a bird that flies on its wings, but they are communities like you.”

Qur’an 6:38

Islam permits meat, yet insists at every turn on mercy (rahma) toward animals and warns against cruelty and waste. Even where a tradition allows flesh, it hedges it with compassion — the same instinct, expressed in a different key.

Isaiah — The peaceable vision

“The wolf shall dwell with the lamb… they shall not hurt nor destroy in all my holy mountain.”

Isaiah 11:6–9

A world without predation is imagined, across traditions, as the ideal — the Satya Yuga of the East and the peaceable kingdom of the West are the same dream. A vegetarian meal is a small, daily rehearsal of it.

Seven scriptures, seven civilisations, one conclusion. This is why TrulyVeg speaks the language of evidence and of every tradition — not of any single one. The case for eating gently is human, not sectarian.

The Ayurvedic Lens

Sattvic, rajasic, tamasic — food shapes the mind

Sattvic — clarity

Fresh grains, fruits, vegetables, milk, ghee, nuts, honey*. Promotes calm, clarity and longevity. This is the food TrulyVeg exists to help you find. (*Honey is accepted by most vegetarian traditions; Jain and vegan readers exclude it.)

Rajasic — agitation

Excessively spicy, sour, salty, fried and stimulating foods. Not forbidden — but Ayurveda links them to restlessness when they dominate a diet.

Tamasic — dullness

Stale, putrid, fermented-spoiled, and flesh foods. Here is the modern insight: synthetic and ultra-processed products fit the tamasic description precisely — lifeless food, far from its source. A packet can be “veg” and still tamasic.

The Modern Evidence

What research says about well-planned vegetarian diets

Large cohort studies and position papers (including from the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics) find that well-planned vegetarian diets are nutritionally adequate for all life stages and associated with measurable health advantages. “Associated with” is honest science language — genetics and lifestyle matter too.

One honest caveat: vegetarians should mind vitamin B12 (supplement or fortified foods), iron, and — if avoiding fish — DHA/EPA omega-3 (algal oil). A samosa-and-cola diet is vegetarian and terrible; the benefits below belong to whole-food vegetarian eating.

Heart health

Vegetarian diets are consistently associated with lower LDL cholesterol, lower blood pressure and reduced rates of ischemic heart disease — fibre up, saturated animal fat down.

Type 2 diabetes

Plant-based eating patterns are linked with better insulin sensitivity and substantially lower incidence of type 2 diabetes in cohort studies.

Healthy weight

Vegetarian populations show lower average BMI; high-fibre plant foods satiate on fewer calories.

Gut microbiome

Diverse plant fibre feeds diverse gut bacteria — increasingly tied to immunity and even mood.

Cancer risk

WHO/IARC classifies processed meat as a Group 1 carcinogen and red meat as Group 2A. A vegetarian plate simply removes the question.

The planet

Producing 1 kg of beef consumes ~15,000 litres of water; 1 kg of wheat ~1,500. Livestock drives a major share of food-system emissions. Every vegetarian meal is a lighter footprint.
