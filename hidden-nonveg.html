The Awareness Page

Don’t eat synthetic products thinking they’re veg.

Two different traps catch honest vegetarians every day. Trap one: animal-derived ingredients hiding behind E-codes and vague words like “enzymes” and “natural flavour”. Trap two: synthetic industrial substances — like acetic-acid “vinegar” — that are technically veg but were never food. This page teaches you to spot both in under a minute per label.

Trap One

Trap Two: “Veg” but synthetic — technically vegetarian, never food.

Synthetic vinegar (industrial acetic acid), dyed “saffron” threads, sodium-hydrosulphite-bleached jaggery, vanaspati passed off as ghee, “analogue paneer” made of starch and refined oil, synthetic colour sweets. None of these contain animals — and none of them deserve a place in a pure kitchen. The vegetarian question is “did an animal suffer for this?”; the shuddh question is “was this ever actually food?” Ask both. The Swap Guide covers each one →

Know Your Marks

What the dots on Indian packs actually mean

🟢

Green dot (in a square)

FSSAI’s mandatory vegetarian mark. Legally means no animal-derived ingredients. Reliable for Indian-made packaged food — but it says nothing about whether the product is natural or synthetic, and it doesn’t exist on imports, medicines or cosmetics.

🟤

Brown/red dot (in a square)

Non-vegetarian — includes egg. If a “paneer roll” pack carries this, something in the seasoning or process is animal-derived. The dot exists precisely because ingredient lists hide things.

🌱

FSSAI vegan logo

The newer green “V” plant logo certifies no animal ingredients and no animal testing. Stricter than the green dot — dairy products can’t carry it. Useful when you also want D3/omega sources resolved.

🌍

Outside India

Most countries have no mandatory veg mark. “Suitable for vegetarians”, Vegetarian Society or V-Label logos are voluntary. No logo = you must read the ingredients yourself. This page is your dictionary.

The Skill

Read any label in 5 steps

Find the veg mark first

In India: green dot present? On imports: any V-label? No mark on an imported product means step 2 is mandatory.

Scan for the big four

Gelatin, carmine/E120, rennet/“enzymes”, shellac/E904. These four catch 80% of accidental non-veg eating worldwide.

Check the yellow codes

E471, E627/E631, E920, “natural flavours”, unspecified D3/omega-3. Ambiguous source — resolved only by a veg mark or the brand’s website.

Ask the synthetic question

Is this made from food? “Acetic acid + water” is not vinegar from food. “Starch + palm oil” is not paneer. If the first ingredient was never grown, find the natural vikalp.

When in doubt, don’t — and write to the brand

Brands answer ingredient-source emails surprisingly fast. Every question you send makes hidden sourcing a little less profitable.

Myth-Busting

“But surely that’s veg…?”

“It’s labelled ‘natural colour’, so it must be from plants.”

No. Carmine — crushed insects — is legally a “natural colour” in most countries. “Natural” describes the extraction, not the kindness. Only a named plant source (beetroot, turmeric, annatto) settles it.

“Cheese is milk, milk is veg, so all cheese is veg.”

The milk is veg; the rennet that sets many cheeses is taken from calf stomachs. Indian cheese is mostly fine; traditional European hard cheeses mostly aren’t. See the cheese comparison.

“My medicine capsule doesn’t count.”

It’s your choice to make — but know that standard capsule shells are gelatin. Tablets and veg-capsule versions of most medicines exist; pharmacists will tell you if you ask. (Never stop a prescribed medicine over the shell — swap formats with your doctor’s help.)

“White sugar is just sugarcane.”

In India, mostly yes (chemically bleached, but veg). In parts of the US, refined sugar is filtered through cattle bone char. If you’re abroad or buying imports, “organic / unrefined / beet sugar” avoids the issue entirely.

“Synthetic vinegar is vegetarian, so what’s the problem?”

It is vegetarian. It’s also diluted industrial acetic acid with zero food heritage, zero nutrition and a harsh one-note taste. Vegetarian is the floor, not the ceiling — VegVikalp’s standard is truly veg and truly food.

“Wine and beer are just fruit and grain.”

Traditional clarification uses isinglass (fish bladder), gelatin, casein or egg white — and it never appears on the label because it’s a “processing aid”. Unfined and vegan-certified options avoid it.
