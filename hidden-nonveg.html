The Awareness Page

Don’t eat synthetic products thinking they’re veg.

Three traps catch honest vegetarians every day, in every country I’ve shopped in. Trap one: animal-derived ingredients hiding behind E-codes and vague words like “enzymes” and “natural flavour”. Trap two: synthetic industrial substances — like acetic-acid “vinegar” — that are technically veg but were never food. Trap three: synthetic and even illegal colours, some made from insects, some from petroleum, some from industrial dyes never meant for the mouth. This page teaches you to spot all three in under a minute per label.

Trap One

A traveller’s rule I trust: the shorter the ingredient list, the fewer places these can hide. A dal, a roti and a seasonal vegetable have no E-codes at all.

Trap Two: “Veg” but synthetic — technically vegetarian, never food.

Synthetic vinegar (industrial acetic acid), dyed “saffron” threads, sodium-hydrosulphite-bleached jaggery, vanaspati passed off as ghee, “analogue paneer” made of starch and refined oil, synthetic colour sweets. None of these contain animals — and none of them deserve a place in a pure kitchen. The vegetarian question is “did an animal suffer for this?”; the shuddh question is “was this ever actually food?” Ask both. The Swap Guide covers each one →

Trap Three

The colour of your food is a chemistry lesson

A bright sweet is not brighter because it is fresher. From the sweet shops of Delhi to the candy aisles of America, the reds, oranges and blues that make food look “fun” are usually one of three things: crushed insects (see E120 above), petroleum-derived dyes born in a factory, or — most dangerous — industrial colours that were never legal in food at all. Nature colours food too, and it does it without any of these.

🧪 Permitted, but synthetic — the petroleum & coal-tar dyes

These are legal in most countries and technically vegetarian — but they are synthesised from petroleum and coal-tar chemistry, not grown. Six of them (marked SB) are the “Southampton Six”: in the EU they must carry the printed warning “may have an adverse effect on activity and attention in children.” Every one has a plant colour that does the same job.

The dangerous ones: illegal colours that were never food dyes at all.

These are not “veg vs non-veg” questions — they are safety questions. In loose, unbranded and street food I have seen textile and industrial dyes used to fake freshness and brightness. They are banned in food everywhere, carry no E-code, and appear on no label — you spot them by suspicion, not by reading. If a colour looks unnaturally vivid on something loose, ask, or walk away.

The kitchen test that never fails: real food does not need to be dyed. A home-made carrot halwa is orange because of carrots; a beetroot cutlet is red because of beetroot. Know Your Marks

What the dots on Indian packs actually mean

Green dot (in a square)

FSSAI’s mandatory vegetarian mark. Legally means no animal-derived ingredients. Reliable for Indian-made packaged food — but it says nothing about whether the product is natural or synthetic, and it doesn’t exist on imports, medicines or cosmetics.

Brown/red dot (in a square)

Non-vegetarian — includes egg. If a “paneer roll” pack carries this, something in the seasoning or process is animal-derived. The dot exists precisely because ingredient lists hide things.

FSSAI vegan logo

The newer green “V” plant logo certifies no animal ingredients and no animal testing. Stricter than the green dot — dairy products can’t carry it. Useful when you also want D3/omega sources resolved.

Outside India

Most countries have no mandatory veg mark. “Suitable for vegetarians”, Vegetarian Society or V-Label logos are voluntary. No logo = you must read the ingredients yourself. This page is your dictionary.

The Skill

Read any label in 5 steps

Find the veg mark first

In India: green dot present? On imports: any V-label? No mark on an imported product means step 2 is mandatory.

Scan for the big four

Gelatin, carmine/E120, rennet/“enzymes”, shellac/E904. These four catch 80% of accidental non-veg eating worldwide.

Check the yellow codes

E471, E627/E631, E920, “natural flavours”, unspecified D3/omega-3. Ambiguous source — resolved only by a veg mark or the brand’s website.

Ask the synthetic question

Is this made from food? “Acetic acid + water” is not vinegar from food. “Starch + palm oil” is not paneer. If the first ingredient was never grown, find the natural alternative.

When in doubt, don’t — and write to the brand

Brands answer ingredient-source emails surprisingly fast. Every question you send makes hidden sourcing a little less profitable.

Myth-Busting

“But surely that’s veg…?”

“It’s labelled ‘natural colour’, so it must be from plants.”

No. Carmine — crushed insects — is legally a “natural colour” in most countries. “Natural” describes the extraction, not the kindness. Only a named plant source (beetroot, turmeric, annatto) settles it.

“Cheese is milk, milk is veg, so all cheese is veg.”

The milk is veg; the rennet that sets many cheeses is taken from calf stomachs. Indian cheese is mostly fine; traditional European hard cheeses mostly aren’t. See the cheese comparison.

“My medicine capsule doesn’t count.”

It’s your choice to make — but know that standard capsule shells are gelatin. Tablets and veg-capsule versions of most medicines exist; pharmacists will tell you if you ask. (Never stop a prescribed medicine over the shell — swap formats with your doctor’s help.)

“White sugar is just sugarcane.”

In India, mostly yes (chemically bleached, but veg). In parts of the US, refined sugar is filtered through cattle bone char. If you’re abroad or buying imports, “organic / unrefined / beet sugar” avoids the issue entirely.

“Synthetic vinegar is vegetarian, so what’s the problem?”

It is vegetarian. It’s also diluted industrial acetic acid with zero food heritage, zero nutrition and a harsh one-note taste. Vegetarian is the floor, not the ceiling — TrulyVeg’s standard is truly veg and truly food.

“Wine and beer are just fruit and grain.”

Traditional clarification uses isinglass (fish bladder), gelatin, casein or egg white — and it never appears on the label because it’s a “processing aid”. Unfined and vegan-certified options avoid it.

“A synthetic colour is only a colour — surely it can’t matter.”

It matters twice. First, some “natural colours” are insects (carmine/E120), so the word natural settles nothing. Second, the petroleum azo dyes — tartrazine, sunset yellow, allura red and their cousins — are legal but carry an activity-and-attention warning for children in the EU for a reason. A colour that comes from a food (turmeric, beetroot, butterfly-pea) is the only one that answers both the veg question and the health question.

“Turmeric is turmeric — how can haldi be a problem?”

Pure turmeric is a golden spice and a medicine. But because its whole value is its colour, it is one of the most adulterated spices on earth — brightened with metanil yellow (a banned dye) or even lead chromate (a toxic metal). Buy whole turmeric fingers and grind them, or stick to lab-tested branded powder. A pinch of pure haldi in water with a few drops of acid stays yellow; if it flushes pink-magenta, it’s adulterated.
